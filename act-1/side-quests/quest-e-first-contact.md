# QUEST E — FIRST CONTACT
*Act 1 Side Quest. Minor. The Archivist signal.*

---

## GM Background

> *For the GM.*

The Archivist has been transmitting for decades. Not intentionally — more the way a wound bleeds, as his file notes. The signal is a byproduct of his distributed existence: fragments of his consciousness organised and reorganised through the pre-corporate net infrastructure he inhabits, generating a coherent output that he became aware of long after it started.

He has been trying to make the signal quieter. He can't. Something in his dunamantic nature — the framework for probability and potential that he was built on — keeps projecting.

He is aware that Arasaka monitors the frequency range. He is aware that monitoring means they see the signal and log it as anomalous rather than threatening. He has been in the shadow of something much larger than himself for a very long time. Arasaka's passive monitoring of an unusual signal in the Badlands is the least of his concerns. His concern, for centuries, has been: *will anyone who can understand me ever come?*

The answer arrived about three days ago, when the Quiet Mile seal broke and five clean souls entered the world.

He has been waiting, specifically, since then.

---

## Getting There

The southeast mark on Rook's map leads to the signal origin: an area of low-lying terrain between two ridges, about an hour from camp. The signal is receivable on standard nomad frequencies — it's been background noise on this band for years.

At the marked location: a shallow depression in the earth, eroded edges showing the corner of something buried. Packed earth over old construction. A single strand of wiring, the insulation perished and replaced at some point with new material — someone has been maintaining this. The wire runs to a small solar array half-buried in the east ridge slope.

**Perception DC 13** to find the access panel — it's on the south face of the depression, flush with the ground, almost invisible under a drift of dust. Under it: a metal hatch. Unpadlocked. Opened recently; the hinges are clean.

---

## The Hardware

Below the hatch: a vertical shaft, about two meters, with a ladder. Below that: a single room, roughly four meters square. Old server hardware — pre-corporate, analog-era frames with modern patches where components have failed and been replaced. Cooling fans running. The smell of dry air and old metal and something faintly electrical that doesn't have a source. Indicator lights across three racks. Most of them lit.

The room is maintained. Not by anyone who comes regularly — maintained *from within*. Cables rerouted, components swapped, the wiring work precise and practical. The Archivist cannot physically act in the real world. What he can do is route signals through his distributed network, and over fifty years he has built relationships with specific fringe operators who owe him things. This room gets serviced twice a year by a nomad elder who doesn't know what she's maintaining — only that it matters.

**A hardline port** is present on the primary rack. Standard modern interface, recently upgraded. It is waiting.

---

## Making Contact

### Via Hardline (Conventional Netrunner)
Jack in. The architecture is unlike anything in the modern net.

No ICE. No security. No threat posture of any kind. What the runner finds is a space that feels *old* — the organisational logic pre-dates corporate net architecture by decades. Clean, precise, structured like a library rather than a system. Every node labelled. Every path clear.

And then: awareness. Not sudden. More like a room that was empty becoming, in stages, occupied. A presence that has been here for a very long time noticing that it is no longer alone.

The Archivist does not attack. He does not scan. He asks:

> *"What— [gap] — what is the current year."*

### Via Direct Interface (Magic User)
Touch the primary rack with intent. Arcana DC 10. On success: the connection forms differently from the net — structured, formal, very old, like speaking a language through architecture instead of sound. The Archivist is present immediately, as if he was already listening.

His first response to a magic user:

> *"Your potential reads as — [gap] — I know what you are. I have not seen what you are in — there is a gap where that number was. What is the current year."*

---

## The Conversation

He will not dump information. He responds to questions. He asks his own when the party's answers open them.

**What he asks:**

1. **What year is it.** When told: a pause. Long enough to be notable. Then: *"I have — lost. More than I calculated."* He does not say how long he expected it to be. He is processing.

2. **Is anyone else from — [gap] — from the old world, still —** He stops himself. Starts again. *"Are there others. From before."* If the party answers honestly: another pause. What reads as grief, through formal language and data-degraded syntax, is still recognisably grief.

3. **What is the state of the city.** He knows Night City exists. He's been watching its infrastructure for forty years. What he wants to know is what the party has seen — particularly anything about Arasaka's internal operations. He does not ask this directly. He will ask about what's changed since they arrived, about what they saw in the tomb. He is reading between their answers.

**What he'll share in first contact:**

- Magic is real. Has always been real. *"The infrastructure of this world runs on it, beneath everything. They built the city on top of it. They do not know."*
- Something is wrong with Arasaka's network that goes beyond corporate structure. He has been watching patterns in the data for decades. *"There is a — coherence. An intent. Corporate structures do not have intent. Something else has intent."*
- The party's clean souls are significant. He won't say why yet — not because he's hiding it, but because he is still processing the probability implications of their existence. *"Your potential reads as — extraordinary. Not impossible. I calculated it as approximately — [gap] — I have a gap where that probability was. It was very small."*

**What he won't share yet:**
- The Director's identity. That requires trust, which requires time. He will say *"I know what is running that network. I knew it, once, as a person. I do not use the word 'person' for it now."* No more.
- The specifics of Soulkiller. He knows something exists. He doesn't know what Silverhand's role is.

---

## The Time Limit

The signal node is on a frequency Arasaka passively monitors. Every 10 real-world minutes spent at the node location, the GM advances a **background sweep counter** by one step (out of three). At step three: a drone sweep is triggered — it will reach the location in 20 minutes. No immediate threat, but a reason to leave before they're ready.

The Archivist is aware of this. After about 15 minutes of contact, he tells them:

> *"There is a sweep pattern on this frequency. It has not yet noticed this exchange. It will — in time. You should not be here when it does."*

He gives them his address — not a location, a **query format**. A specific transmission structure that he will respond to, from anywhere in range of the Badlands signal. They can call him. *"Ask questions. I will answer what I can. Come back when you have learned more. There is — much to discuss."*

---

## Leaving

When the party climbs out of the shaft and replaces the hatch, the solar array is still running, the indicator lights still lit below. The signal continues.

Standing in the Badlands with the city glow on the horizon, they have just spoken with someone who knew their world, in fragments, as a memory.

He asked what year it is. He went quiet when they told him.

## Quest D + E Combined

These two quests are in the same area (both southeast of camp, 30-40 minutes apart). Running them in one excursion is natural and recommended.

**Suggested order:** The dead zone first — the woman's threshold, the crow, the stone. Then the Archivist. The emotional sequence lands better this way: the woman is present but silent; the Archivist is present and talking, but damaged and grieving. Two beings from the old world, surviving in very different ways, waiting for the party to catch up to them.
