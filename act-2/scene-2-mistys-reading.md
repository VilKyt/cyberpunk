# SCENE 2 — MISTY'S READING
*Act 2, Scene 2*

---

## GM Background

> *For the GM only.*

Misty Olszewski runs an esoteric shop in Watson — crystals, incense, tarot, the kind of place Night City tolerates the way it tolerates anything that doesn't cost it money. She is not a gimmick. She is genuinely perceptive, and her readings land in ways she cannot entirely explain, which she has accepted without drama. She does not perform mysticism. She receives it and passes it on.

Jackie Welles was her partner. He died recently — the Konpeki Plaza job, the heist, the way these things go in Night City. She is still in the part of grief where you work because stopping would be worse. The shop is orderly. She is orderly. The cards are what they have always been.

She has another client — V — who she reads for regularly. V is currently somewhere in the city, running out of time in a way Misty understands better than she lets on. V has not told her about the chip. Misty has not asked. The cards have been saying something strange for months and she is not sure she wants to name it.

**The party is going to be the strangest reading she has ever done.** The cards will respond to them differently than to anyone she has read for before — more clearly, more insistently, like a signal with no interference. She will be unsettled in a quiet, professional way. She will complete the reading. She will not pretend it was routine.

---

## Getting There

The shop is below the main street noise of Watson — incense and low light, the specific silence of a room where people come when things have gone wrong. Misty is behind the counter when they arrive. She looks at them for a moment.

She will read for them. She does not explain why she offers — she just does, the way she offers to most people who come in looking like they've walked out of something they don't have words for yet.

She uses a five-card spread, positional. She lays the cards without theatrics.

---

## The Spread

```
        [ 4 ]
 [ 2 ]  [ 1 ]  [ 3 ]
        [ 5 ]
```

| Position | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 — Centre | What you are |
| 2 — Left | What you seek |
| 3 — Right | What threatens you |
| 4 — Above | What stands against you |
| 5 — Below | The price already paid — and still being paid |

She lays them face-down in order. Turns them one at a time. Reads as she goes.

---

## Card 1 — Centre — THE FOOL

**What you are.**

She turns it. Looks at it. Looks at them. Looks back at the card.

> *"I've never pulled this for a group before. It usually comes up for someone at the very beginning of something — before they've learned the rules, before the world has had a chance to get its hands on them."*

> *"You came from very far away. And I don't mean distance."*

She doesn't elaborate. The card sits at the centre of the spread while she moves on. But she keeps glancing back at it.

**The Fool** — *upright*

The figure stepping off the cliff, pack on their back, unconcerned. Outside of time. Operating on different rules than the world below. The drop is real. The Fool doesn't know it's there, which means the fall hasn't happened yet. In the context of this party: they are genuinely outside the systems that run Night City. They don't carry the soul-drain's trace. They don't know the rules. That is not only a vulnerability. It is also, in ways nobody has fully mapped, a kind of power.

> *GM note: any magic-sensitive player who looks at the card notices something odd — the figure in the image seems to be looking back at them rather than forward. This is not a mechanical effect. It's flavour. But it's worth playing.*

---

## Card 2 — Left — JUDGEMENT

**What you seek.**

She turns it. The classic image: an angel, a trumpet, the dead rising from graves to answer a call that should be impossible.

> *"Someone who is gone. Someone who should be gone — completely gone — but isn't. Not entirely."*

A pause.

> *"There's a man in here who burned everything. His whole life was a fight against something he could feel but couldn't name. He died for it. That should have been the end."*

She traces the edge of the card.

> *"The cards are telling you to find him. What's left of him. I don't know what that means — I've never read for someone being sent to find the dead before."*

She doesn't know she is describing Johnny Silverhand. She doesn't know about the chip in V's head. She is reading what the cards show her, which is a man who died in a fight he understood better than he knew, whose refusal to fully leave is not a malfunction but a necessity.

**Judgement** — *upright*

Resurrection. A call from beyond a final state. Something that should have ended answering instead. The card traditionally speaks to reckoning and awakening — here it speaks to a soul that exists in a state that shouldn't be possible, and is exactly what the party needs because of it.

> *GM note: the party doesn't know yet who this is. "Find someone who died for this fight" is a thread they'll need to pull. The trail — through Silverhand's legend, through Rogue, through the old records — eventually leads to V. Don't short-circuit that search. Let them work for the realisation.*

---

## Card 3 — Right — THE MOON

**What threatens you.**

She turns it. The moon over dark water. A dog and a wolf at the shoreline. Something half-submerged in the pool at the bottom of the card.

She is quiet for a moment before she speaks.

> *"The city runs on something you can't see. A tide. It goes in — and when it comes back, it comes back a little less full."*

> *"You're clean right now. Whatever you are, whatever brought you here — you came in clean. The cards are glad you're clean."*

Her voice doesn't change register but the next line lands differently.

> *"Nothing stays clean in Night City without tending. The tide doesn't ask. It just takes. And you don't notice until you look back and the rock is exposed, and you can't remember when the sand was there."*

She does not say: *your souls will erode.* She doesn't have that vocabulary. But she is telling them exactly that.

**The Moon** — *upright*

Hidden danger. Things that operate in the dark, below the surface, without announcement. The tidal pull of something that acts passively and constantly. The wolf and the dog at the waterline: the danger is both wild and domesticated, both obvious and unrecognisable. The Moon is associated with water, with tides — the erosion metaphor is built into the card itself. She is warning them that what threatens them is not a predator. It is a property of the environment.

> *GM note: this is the direct hook toward the warded ink at the tattoo parlor, though Misty doesn't know that. She is describing the soul-drain accurately. If the players have already visited the Artist, they will recognise exactly what she means. If they haven't yet — the reading is a reason to seek that scene.*

---

## Card 4 — Above — DEATH (reversed)

**What stands against you.**

She turns it upside down. Holds it a moment before placing it.

> *"This one—"*

She stops. Starts again.

> *"When this card comes up the right way, it means change. Transformation. The end of one thing so something else can begin."*

> *"Reversed — it means something that won't end. Something locked in a state that should be temporary. An old thing. Very patient. It has been waiting a long time for something, and whatever it's waiting for — I think it might be you."*

She does not say Arasaka. She does not say corporation. She says:

> *"Behind whatever face you see — there is something that should have finished a long time ago. It didn't. It won't. Not on its own."*

She taps the card once. Sets it above the spread. Does not look at it again.

**Death** — *reversed*

Stagnation. Refusal to transform. A state of undeath — the process halted, the ending denied, something suspended in a form it should have moved through and left behind. Without naming the Director, without any framework the party has introduced, Misty has read him correctly: an ancient intelligence locked in a form that refuses to conclude, waiting with the patience of something that has already outlasted everything it ever knew.

> *GM note: do not name Arasaka here. Do not name the Director. The reading should leave the party with the image — an old thing, very patient, locked in something that should have ended — and let them place it themselves. If they haven't connected the corporation to the lich yet, this is a seed. If they have, it lands as confirmation.*

---

## Card 5 — Below — TEN OF SWORDS

**The price already paid — and still being paid.**

She turns the last card.

The image: a figure face-down, ten swords in the back. The sky at the horizon, just beginning to lighten.

Misty goes still. Just for a moment.

> *"This card came up in a reading I did recently."*

She doesn't say whose reading. She doesn't say the name. She looks at the dawn in the background of the image for a few seconds — the thin line of orange at the horizon, the swords, the figure who has already taken everything that was coming.

> *"People always think this is the worst card in the deck. It isn't. It means the worst thing has already happened. And the sun is still coming up."*

A beat.

> *"I don't know if that's comforting. I've been trying to decide."*

She does not say Jackie. She will not say Jackie. But she is thinking about him, and the card is telling her the same thing it told her then — that an absolute ending is not the same as the absence of a future. That someone fell here so that the horizon is still there.

The position of this card is *below* — foundation, root, what the reading rests on. The price is not a warning about the future. It is structural. Something has already been paid. Something will be paid again.

**Ten of Swords** — *upright*

Absolute defeat. The lowest point. But the card's image always includes the dawn — this is not nihilism, it is the specific acknowledgement that the worst thing and the continuation of things can coexist. In this reading: a sacrifice has already been made (Jackie, though the party doesn't know this). More will be asked. The card is not trying to prevent this. It is telling them that the cost is real, and that it matters, and that something survives it.

> *GM note: this card should land emotionally even before the party knows who or what it refers to. Misty's response to it is visible — she contains it, but it costs her something. That tells the players this card has weight beyond the abstract. It also seeds: there is another reading in play somewhere in the city, and it has the same card in it.*

---

## Between Cards 3 and 4 — The Interruption

Midway through the reading — after The Moon, before she reaches for the fourth card — Misty's phone buzzes against the counter.

She glances at it. Stands. Quiet apology:

> *"One moment."*

She goes through the curtain at the back of the shop.

The party hears, faintly, a phone call: one voice, tired, practical, the specific flatness of someone running on insufficient sleep. Then, briefly — a different register. Rougher. More angular. Not a different caller. The same call. But a different voice underneath the first one, or beside it, or inside it.

Both voices stop. Misty comes back. She sits down and picks up the fourth card without explaining.

If anyone asks who that was: *"A client."* That is all she will say.

> *GM note: a magic-sensitive player who pays attention knows two things — there were two voices on that call, and one of them was not entirely present in the same way the other was. Don't confirm or explain this in-scene. Let it sit.*

---

## After the Reading

She gathers the cards. She doesn't hurry. She is thinking.

If the party asks questions:

**On the dead man they need to find:**
> *"I don't know who it is. The cards don't give names. They gave me the shape of someone — a fight, a refusal, something that should have ended and didn't. That's all I have."*

**On the threat:**
> *"Protect yourselves. Whatever you came in as — don't let Night City thin you out. People have been thinned out by this city for decades. I've read for a lot of them. They don't notice while it's happening."*

**On the ancient thing behind everything:**
> *"I see it sometimes in readings for people who work close to the towers. Something in the background. I always thought it was interference. I don't think that anymore."*

**On the last card:**
> *"I hope the worst has already happened to you. I genuinely do."* 

She means it.
